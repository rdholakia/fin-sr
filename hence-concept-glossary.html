Layer 1 · Content itemsThe plan, the moves, and the messages Hence generates.

What is Ask?

Ask is the primary conversational surface. You can query your own data or bring a bigger question and start a new Path — logged in or out.

It's the front door: most Goals and Paths begin here.

Qualities
  • Two modes: query your own data, or ask an open question.
  • Can start a new Path directly from chat.
  • Available logged-out (with sample personas) and logged-in.
  • Supports showing up without a question — "am I doing life right?"
Design principles
  • Answer the question under the question — reframe to the real problem before gathering data.
  • Voice over forms where you can.
  • Force-feed the medicine — surface the five or six moves they should make, not just the one they asked about.
  • Take a clear position; egoless delivery, no flattery, no judgment.
  • Make background work visible, and hand over a pre-loaded last step.

What is a Guide?

A Guide is a step-by-step program through a common life event or a financial area, with scenario-planning and trade-off tools to right-size your Goals.

Qualities
  • Two families:
    • Situational: Start a Family · Rent vs. Buy · Buy a Home · Retirement · Lost My Job · Career Change/Break.
    • Financial-area (Planners): Emergency Fund Sizer · Cash Margin Calculator · Debt Pay-down Calculator · Insurance Check-up · Retirement Income Planner.
  • A Planner is a Guide focused on one Financial Area, built around a calculator or sizer.
  • May establish a Goal, add Tasks to a Path, or start a new Path.
Design principles
  • Right-size, don't prescribe — use trade-off tools so the user sets the target.
  • Carry a house POV (a clear recommendation), not a neutral calculator.
  • Hand off into a Goal + Path when the user commits.

What is a Goal?

A Goal is a specific outcome you're working toward — a life milestone (buy a house, get set up before the baby, build a three-month cushion) or the resolution of a money question you brought to Hence (should I refinance?).

Every Goal has a finish line. Hence creates a Path — the work to move you toward it; you take only the actions that have to be yours.

Qualities
  • Originates from an Ask (a question the user raised) or a Guide (a Hence-initiated recommendation tied to a weak area). This can include the Financial Foundation.
  • Finite — they have a defined completion state.
    • Associated with a single Path (an ordered series of Checkpoints).
    • Complete = all Checkpoints and/or Tasks on its Path are met.
    • Can be associated with ≥1 Financial Foundation Area, connecting the goal to improved financial health.
  • States: Active, Workshopping, Pending.
Design principles
  • Goals should be personalized — derived from the user's actual situation, not a generic template.
  • Goals should always have clear, measurable milestones (Checkpoints). No Goal without a defined finish and intermediate markers.
  • Hence should explain why the goal is important — connect the money move to the life decision.
  • Hence should draw the line between what it handles and what only the user can do. This is the Champion contract: Hence drafts / prices / maps / queues; the user opens / enrolls / submits.
  • If it can't be finished, it isn't a Goal — it's an area-health concern or an ongoing behavior. The finite test is the gate.

What is a Path?

An ordered series of Checkpoints that moves you to a Goal.

Qualities
  • Exactly one Path per Goal.
  • An ordered sequence of Checkpoints.
  • Started by an Ask, a Guide, or adding a Goal.
  • Small Goals may have no Checkpoints — only Services and/or Tasks.
Design principles
  • Keep it the single spine of a Goal — no parallel Paths.
  • Open: with one Path per Goal, Path may be redundant with Goal — confirm whether it survives as its own object.

What is a Checkpoint?

A Checkpoint is a measurable milestone on a Path — a state you reach. Tasks are the actions that complete it.

It can be a fiscal threshold (an amount reached, e.g. $2,000 in savings) or an action-based milestone (something done, e.g. insurance switched).

Qualities
  • Two kinds: a fiscal threshold (an amount reached) or an action-based milestone (a state achieved).
  • Completed by Tasks — the actions that add up to it. A Checkpoint is not itself a Task. (Corrects the source's "a checkpoint can be a Task" wording.)
  • Auto-created when a Path is added to your Overview.
  • Can sit on more than one Path.
  • Some Checkpoints also have Services.
  • ⚠ Open — the single-action case (for group review): when a milestone needs only one action, either —
    • Option A — collapse to the Checkpoint: keep just the Checkpoint, skip the lone Task (no one-item nesting). Trade-off: a Checkpoint can be satisfied directly, blurring milestone-vs-action slightly.
    • Option B — keep them mutually exclusive: the action stays a Task on the Path with no Checkpoint. Trade-off: cleaner definitions, but a Path then holds two kinds of thing (Checkpoints and standalone Tasks).
Design principles
  • Always measurable — a Checkpoint you can't verify isn't one.
  • Tasks are the unit of action; the Checkpoint is the unit of progress they roll up to. Keep them distinct, not interchangeable.
  • Reuse across Paths where the same milestone serves two Goals (e.g. an emergency fund shared by Start-a-Family and Buy-a-Home).

What is a Task?

An incremental action you or your agents take. Tasks are how Checkpoints get completed.

Qualities
  • Singular. Recurring tasks are Services.
  • The actions that complete a Checkpoint — grouped as the steps that add up to it.
  • May stand alone on a Path only in the single-action case — and only under Option B (see Checkpoint).
  • Taken by the user or by an agent.
Design principles
  • If it recurs, it's a Service, not a Task — cadence is the test.
  • A Task is an action, not a milestone — don't let it stand in for a Checkpoint.
  • Draw the Champion line: agent Tasks vs. the user Tasks only you can submit.

What is a Service?

An ongoing agentic routine Hence runs to monitor and support your financial health or complete a Goal — re-shopping insurance, benchmarking income, sizing the emergency fund, watching for homes in range.

Qualities
  • Recurring by nature — recurring Tasks are Services.
  • A child of a Financial Area.
  • Some are permanent parts of an Area; some are localized to a Goal and turn off when it completes.
  • A Goal-localized Service graduates to its Financial Area when the Goal is marked complete.
  • Generated by an Ask, a Planner, or the Money Map flow.
Design principles
  • Make the background work visible — an unseen Service gets turned off ("I checked 250 carriers; none beat your rate").
  • Always attributable to a Financial Area.

What is a Check-in?

A recurring report on your Plan — weekly, monthly, or quarterly.

Qualities
  • Contains a high-level summary, agent actions taken, data requests, prepared to-dos, and proactive suggestions.
  • Cadence: a monthly check-in is the working default (max); possibly a ramp.
  • Delivered in-product or by email.
Design principles
  • Three questions, once a month — new balances, new facts, anything changed.
  • Lead with what Hence handled, then what needs the user.

What is a Financial Area?

One of the five areas Hence reviews to answer "How am I doing?" and "How could I be doing better?" Scored by how well your picture matches your right-sized benchmarks.

Names and metrics are from Dawn's Metric Framework v1.0.

Qualities
  • The five (in display order): Cash Flow, Stability, Debt, Protection, The Long Game.
    • Cash Flow — "where the money goes": Monthly Commitments + Breathing Room. → Budget Breakdown.
    • Stability — "if the paycheck stopped": Months at Full / Lean Spend. → Savings Health.
    • Debt — "what you owe" (non-mortgage): Debt Weight + Debt Drag. → Debt Assessment.
    • Protection — "to weather the unexpected": Coverage Gaps + Coverage Strength. → Protection Health.
    • The Long Game — Income Replacement, Savings Pace, Work-Optional Age, Investment Balance. → Retirement Readiness.
  • Income is not an area — it's the one required input. Gross income powers the Long Game (savings pace, projection) and, via take-home, Cash Flow / Stability / Debt.
  • Most Goals touch more than one Area. Services are children of Areas. Each Area carries a score and a confidence.
  • ⚠ Open: name fork only (Area vs Pillar). The five-vs-six question is now resolved by Dawn's framework.
Design principles
  • Score against right-sized benchmarks (sized by Money Mindset), not generic targets.
  • Never fake a number — show grey until there's enough data.
  • Show data provenance and a way to raise confidence.

What are the Area Assessments?

The rendered assessment of each Financial Area — the screen that answers "how am I doing here, and what's next." One per area (metrics from Dawn's framework).

Qualities
  • Budget Breakdown — Monthly Commitments + Breathing Room → Cash Flow.
  • Savings Health — Months at Full / Lean Spend → Stability.
  • Debt Assessment — Debt Weight + Debt Drag → Debt.
  • Protection Health — Coverage Gaps + Coverage Strength → Protection.
  • Retirement Readiness — Income Replacement, Work-Optional Age, Investment Balance → The Long Game.
  • Income Assessment — earnings benchmark. Cross-cutting input, not an area — feeds Cash Flow (take-home) and the Long Game (gross → savings pace, projection).
  • (ALT: Emergency Readiness — a floated merge of Stability + Protection, not adopted.)
Design principles
  • Show the headline metric, then the benchmark band (e.g. 3 mo / 6 mo).
  • Give a mindset-aware "what to do next" ("since your Money Mindset prefers security…").
  • State the confidence and the data behind it ("Data: paystub + two months of statements"); offer to raise it ("improve confidence with 3 more questions").

How is an Area scored?

How an Area gets a score, and how sure Hence is of it.

Qualities
  • Confidence Level: every Area carries a score and a confidence.
  • Data tiers: Tier 1a/1b/1c compute from onboarding alone (gross income is the one required field); Tier 2 unlocks precise metrics with account-level data.
  • Grey state: insufficient data shows grey with a "tell us X, we'll show Y" prompt.
  • Right-sized benchmark: the personalized target, sized by Money Mindset, above hard safety floors.
  • Savings Pace: contributions ÷ gross; ≥15% green — scored in Long Game.
Design principles
  • Never a partial or fabricated number — grey is honest.
  • Make raising confidence a clear, low-friction action.

What is the Personalization layer?

The dials that tune how Hence talks to you — held lightly, since the research didn't ground self-perception against actual behavior.

Qualities
  • Advice complexity ← financial literacy.
  • Tone ← mirror how the person speaks.
  • Belief about money → a fit / ICP filter, not a dial.
  • Direction vs. exploration — a list, or explore it together.
  • Risk — held lightly.
Design principles
  • Mirror the user's register; don't impose a house voice.
  • Use belief-fit to decide who the product is for, not to argue someone out of their worldview.

Who Hence is for, and the core reframe.

Qualities
